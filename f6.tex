\section{Lecture 6}
\subsection{Substitution Method}
We want to maximize / minimize \( f\left( x,y \right) \) with constraint \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). We can solve \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \) for one variable \( y=y\left( x \right) \).
\medskip

\begin{eg}
	Consider \( f\left( x,y \right) = x^2 + y^2 \) and \( g\left( x,y \right) = xy-1 =0\). In this case, we have \( f=\frac{1}{x}  \) from \( g = 0 \), we get

	\[ h(x)=f\left( x,x^{-1} \right) = x^2+x^{-2} \]

	We have found the minima at \( \left( 1,1 \right) \text{ and } \left( -1,-1 \right) \).
\end{eg}

This method isn't always feasible, so lets look at some alternatives.

In the best case, \( f\left( x,y \right) = c \) is just touching \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). If this is worked out geometrically, we get \( \left( 1,1 \right) \text{ and } \left( -1,-1 \right) \).
\end{eg}
\bigskip

This idea is used in Lagranges method. We want \( f\left( x,y \right)=c \) to be parallell to \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). More precisesly: Their vectors should be parallell.

Equivalently, their normal vectors are also paralell. Recall that a normal vector to \( g=0 \) is given by \( \nabla f \). Similarly, \( \nabla f \) is normal to \( f=c \).


\subsection{Method for Langrange}
Suppose we want to find a local maxima and minima of \( f\left( x,y \right) \) with constraint \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). We proceed as follows

\begin{enumerate}
	\item Find all possible solutions to the equations

	      \[ \nabla f\left( x,y \right)= \lambda\nabla g\left( x,y \right), \quad g\left( x,y \right)=0 \]

	\item Plug in all solutions from 1. into \( f\left( x,y \right) \) and identify the largest and smallest.

	      \begin{itemize}
		      \item The number \( \lambda \) is called the \textit{Lagrange Multiplier}.
		      \item Easy extension to \( n \) variables.
		      \item Can also be generalized to multiple constraints, \( g_{1}, \ldots , g_{n} \)
	      \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

\begin{eg}
	We have the following functions

	\[ f\left( x,y \right)=x^2+y^2, \quad g\left( x,y \right) = xy-1 = 0 \]

	We compute the gradients

	\[ \nabla f\left( x,y \right)=\left( 2x,2y \right), \quad \nabla g\left( x,y \right)=\left( x,y \right) \]

	The Lagrange equiations are

	\[ 2x=\lambda y, \quad 2y = \lambda x ,\quad xy-1 = 0 \]

	Observe that \( \left( x,y,\lambda \right) = \left( 0,0,0 \right) \) is not a solution. For \( x \neq 0 \), we get \( y = \frac{1}{x}  \), from the third equation.

	\begin{align*}
		 & 2x = \lambda y \Rightarrow 2x = \lambda \frac{1}{x} \Rightarrow \lambda = 2x^2 \\
		 & 2y = \lambda x \Rightarrow \frac{2}{x} = 2xs^2 \cdot x \Rightarrow x^{4} = 1   \\
		 & x^{4} = 1 \Rightarrow x \pm 1, \quad y = \frac{1}{x}
	\end{align*}

	\[ \left( x,y \right) = \left( 1,1 \right) \quad\wedge\quad \left( x,y \right)  = \left( -1,-1 \right)\]

	Are theses points the minima? We have

	\[ f\left( 1,1 \right) = f\left( -1,-1 \right) = 2 \]

	Lets Compare this to some other points, such that \( g\left( x,y \right) = 0 \). For instance, \( \left( x,y \right) = \left( 2,\frac{1}{2}  \right) \)

	\[ g\left( 2, \frac{1}{2}  \right) = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} -1 = 0 \]

	\[ f\left( 2, \frac{1}{2}  \right) = 2^2 + \left( \frac{1}{2}  \right)^2 > 2 \]

	This tells us that \( \left( x,y \right) = \left( \pm1, \pm1 \right) \) are local minima.
\end{eg}
