\section{Lecture 6}
\subsection{Substitution Method}
We want to maximize / minimize \( f\left( x,y \right) \) with constraint \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). We can solve \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \) for one variable \( y=y\left( x \right) \).
\medskip

\begin{eg}
	Consider \( f\left( x,y \right) = x^2 + y^2 \) and \( g\left( x,y \right) = xy-1 =0\). In this case, we have \( f=\frac{1}{x}  \) from \( g = 0 \), we get

	\[ h(x)=f\left( x,x^{-1} \right) = x^2+x^{-2} \]

	We have found the minima at \( \left( 1,1 \right) \text{ and } \left( -1,-1 \right) \).
\end{eg}

This method isn't always feasible, so lets look at some alternatives.

\subsection{Lagrange's Method}
\begin{eg}
	Lets look at the level curves, which are circles. We have the that \( f\left( x,y \right) = x^2 + y^2 = c \).

	Smaller circles correspont to smaller values of \( f\left( x,y \right) \), but we must also satisfy \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). In the best case, \( f\left( x,y \right) = c \) is just touching \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \). If this is worked out geometrically, we get \( \left( 1,1 \right) \text{ and } \left( -1,-1 \right) \).
\end{eg}
\bigskip

This idea is used in Lagranges method. We want \( f\left( x,y \right)=c \) to be parallell to \( g\left( x,y \right)=0 \)
